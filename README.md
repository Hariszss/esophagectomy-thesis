# Staged ML Prediction of Postoperative Complications After Esophagectomy

Analysis code and notebooks from my MSc Applied Data Science thesis (Utrecht University),
carried out in collaboration with the Department of Surgery and the Department of
Rehabilitation Medicine, Amsterdam UMC. The thesis builds a staged preoperative prediction
model for postoperative complications after esophagectomy, using the Amsterdam UMC
institutional cohort of the Dutch Upper GI Cancer Audit (DUCA, 2016–2025, N=1,181), and
evaluates whether preoperative physiotherapy assessment and ICF-coded functioning add
predictive value on top of the registry-based model.

## Research question

How well can postoperative complications after esophagectomy be predicted from
preoperative information, and does discrimination improve once the preoperative
physiotherapy assessment and ICF-coded functioning are added? Predictors are organised
into four cumulative stages — demographics and fitness (Stage 1), comorbidity and
lifestyle (Stage 2), tumour and neoadjuvant treatment characteristics (Stage 3), and
surgical factors (Stage 4) — evaluated with LASSO logistic regression, Random Forest,
and XGBoost under 5-fold stratified cross-validation. SHAP values are used to quantify
predictor contributions on the best-performing model. As a supplementary analysis, ICF
(International Classification of Functioning) scores extracted from clinical notes are
linked to the surgical cohort to characterise patient functioning before and after surgery,
and tested for incremental predictive value.

## Data

No data are included in this repository. All analyses were run inside a secure research
environment (MyDRE), and the source data never leave that environment.

Data sources referenced in the code:

## Notebooks

| Notebook | Purpose |
|---|---|
| `01_preprocessing` | Builds the DUCA esophagectomy cohort (N=1,181), recodes registry sentinel values to missing, imputes, and derives the outcomes (any complication, Clavien-Dindo grade, major complication). |
| `02_descriptive_statistics` | Cohort description and outcome prevalence (57.6% with any complication) reported in the thesis tables. |
| `03_staged_prediction_model` | The four-stage cumulative model, LASSO/Random Forest/XGBoost under 5-fold stratified cross-validation, hyperparameter sensitivity, and SHAP analysis on the best-performing model. |
| `04_icf_perioperative_analysis` | Pre- vs post-operative ICF functioning scores across 17 domains (paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests), N=533 patients with a preoperative ICF note. |
| `05_icf_prediction_value` | Physiotherapist preoperative risk classification accuracy vs the data-driven model, selection bias in the ICF-assessed subgroup, and ICF incremental predictive value. |

## Methods

Models are evaluated with 5-fold stratified cross-validation; imputation and scaling are
fit within each training fold to avoid leakage. Discrimination is reported as the
cross-validated area under the ROC curve. SHAP values quantify predictor contributions on
the Stage 4 Random Forest. ICF pre/post comparisons use paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests.

## Key findings

- Best-performing model: Random Forest at Stage 4 (AUC = 0.615), with LASSO logistic regression performing comparably (AUC = 0.612).
- Surgical factors added at Stage 4 gave the largest incremental gain in discriminative performance.
- SHAP identified anastomosis location, operation duration, BMI, histology, and ASA score as the dominant predictors.
- Most physical ICF domains worsened post-operatively (exercise tolerance Δ = −0.64, respiration Δ = −0.25), while eating function improved the most (Δ = +0.66).
- The physiotherapist's preoperative risk classification (AUC = 0.536) was substantially weaker than the data-driven model in the same subset (AUC = 0.629).
- Preoperative ICF scores did not meaningfully improve model performance, likely due to limited sample size and non-random data availability.

## Reproducibility

The notebooks are provided so the analysis steps and modelling choices can be inspected
and reproduced on equivalent data. The source data are not provided and cannot be
redistributed, so the notebooks are not runnable end to end without access to the DUCA
registry through the appropriate governance route. Notebook outputs have been cleared
before publishing.
